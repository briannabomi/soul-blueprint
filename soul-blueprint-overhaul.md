# Soul Blueprint Experience — Full UX & Copy Overhaul

**Date:** April 26, 2026
**Purpose:** Clarity, conversion, and UX overhaul based on real user feedback
**Design principle:** Optimize like a $10K product converting a cold but high-quality lead

## Part 1: Brutal Feedback — What's Hurting Conversion Right Now

### The 3-second test: FAILING

A first-time visitor landing on this page has to parse "five ancient systems," "decode why your soul incarnated," and "honors that knowing" before they understand what they're getting. That's a cognitive tax on people who are already skeptical. You lose them before the button.

### What's self-indulgent vs. useful

**Self-indulgent (cut or rewrite):**

- "The moment you arrived carries information your mind has never been taught to read." — This sounds profound to the creator. To a cold lead, it sounds like a riddle they didn't sign up for. It's Step 1. They haven't earned this language yet and they haven't opted into it.
- "cosmic coordinates" (used 3x) — This is insider language. It signals "this is for people who already think like me," which is the opposite of what a lead magnet should do.
- "honor that knowing" — Vague. Knowing what? Honor how? This is a sentence that creates a feeling in someone who already agrees with you and creates confusion in everyone else.
- "Your Soul Blueprint is ready to be woven. Where should I send it?" — Too precious. The user just answered 12 questions. Don't get poetic at the finish line. Get them across it.
- "Weaving your Soul Blueprint..." / "Synthesizing five ancient systems into one portrait" — Processing messages that sound like a spiritual ceremony. These should feel like a machine working, not a ritual happening.
- "Not what your mind chose to pursue. What your soul encoded into the mathematics of your birth." — Report-section reframes. These are good *in moderation*. Currently there's one before almost every section. By the third one, they feel like a verbal tic.
- "Name the thing your soul keeps circling back to" (placeholder text) — Assumes the user thinks in soul-language. Most of your ICP does not. Yet.

**Useful (keep and amplify):**

- "Not what your mind wants. Not what your fear will tolerate. What your soul is actually asking for." — This is the single best line in the experience. It's clear, it's sharp, it creates instant recognition in the right person.
- "Purpose without infrastructure is a journal entry." — Direct, smart, memorable. This is the line that makes a skeptic lean in.
- The ikigai frame names (What You Love / Good At / World Needs / Paid For) — Clear, universal, zero learning curve.
- The Gene Key shadow → gift → siddhi spectrum visual — Elegant, immediately graspable.
- The Sovereign Arc CTA at the end — Well-structured bridge from free value to paid work.

### What should be deleted entirely

- The "Essence Extraction Engine" output in the report — the "Recurring Threads," "Core Drives," "Unique Signatures," "Language Patterns," and "Purpose Pillars" sections. This is *massively* over-engineered for a lead magnet. It produces 5+ additional report sections based on keyword-matching against short-form textarea answers. The analysis looks sophisticated but the input data is too thin to support it. Someone who types 15 words into "What do you love?" doesn't need a "Diagnostic Transformer" archetype label and a "somatic regulation" interpretation. It makes the report feel like it's projecting onto the user rather than seeing them. **Recommendation: Cut the entire essence engine from the report. Keep the raw ikigai answers displayed simply. Let the five-system synthesis do the heavy lifting.**
- Step count inconsistency — The progress says "Step 1 of 7" but the email screen says "Step 6 of 6." This is a trust-breaker.
- The "tone profile" analysis ("Hedging: your language softens...") — Analyzing someone's writing *tone* from textarea answers and then diagnosing them feels invasive and presumptuous in a free tool.

### Where users would drop off

1. **Step 1 (birth data)** — "Your Cosmic Coordinates" headline + "The moment you arrived carries information your mind has never been taught to read" = immediate cognitive overload. The user came to learn about their purpose. They're now being told their birth carries encoded information. That's a leap they haven't agreed to make yet.
2. **Ikigai Step 1 (What You Love)** — 3 open-ended textareas with no examples. Cold leads stare at blank boxes. Some close the tab.
3. **Ikigai Step 3 (What the World Needs)** — "What do you believe would make the world a better place?" is so broad it paralyzes. Combined with "Name the thing your soul keeps circling back to" as placeholder text, this is where thoughtful people freeze.
4. **Ikigai Step 4 (What You Can Be Paid For)** — "Don't filter for what's 'realistic.' Name what's true." The user just shifted from emotional/idealistic questions to a money question, and you're telling them not to be realistic about money. Feels disorienting.
5. **Email capture** — "Your Soul Blueprint is ready to be woven" asks for poetic engagement at the moment of highest friction (giving up an email). Users want speed here, not ceremony.

## Part 4: UX Recommendations

### Progress bar

**Current state:** A 2px bar exists but it's barely visible and the step counts are inconsistent (says "Step 1 of 7" on birth data, "Step 6 of 6" on email).

**Recommendation:**
- Increase bar height to 4px.
- Fix step count to a consistent **6 steps**: Birth Data → What You Love → What You're Good At → What the World Needs → What You Can Be Paid For → Email/Generate.
- Add a **percentage label** next to the bar: "Step 2 of 6" is good, but also consider adding the section name: "Step 2 of 6 — What you love"
- Processing screens don't count as steps. They're transitions.

### Question count

**Current:** 12 ikigai questions (3 per section × 4 sections) + 4 birth data fields + 2 email fields = 18 total inputs.

**Recommended:** 8 ikigai questions (2 per section × 4 sections) + 4 birth data fields + 2 email fields = 14 total inputs.

**Rationale:** The third question in each ikigai section is the weakest and most redundant. "What makes you feel most alive and energized?" overlaps with "What activities make you lose track of time?" The extra question adds ~2 minutes and increases the likelihood of fatigue-driven abandonment. Two strong questions per section is enough signal for the analysis engine and the report synthesis.

### Skip logic

**Where to allow skipping:** Every ikigai section should have a "Not sure? Skip this section" link. It should be a subtle text link below the Continue button (not a competing button).

**What skipping means technically:** If a user skips, the report should gracefully handle empty ikigai quadrants — display "You chose to skip this section" in the ikigai grid and exclude that quadrant from the cross-signal analysis. The synthesis section should still work with partial data.

**Do NOT allow skipping:** Birth data (it's required for calculations) and email (it's the conversion point — but offer a "skip, just show results" option so you don't lose the person entirely).

### Completion time framing

**Current:** "about 10 minutes of honesty with yourself"

**Recommended:** "Takes about 8 minutes."

Why 8: It's more precise (feels researched), it's under 10 (psychological threshold), and dropping "of honesty with yourself" removes the implied emotional labor.

### Mobile considerations

- Current max-width of 720px works well for mobile.
- Textarea inputs should have a slightly taller min-height on mobile (100px instead of 80px) — thumbs need room.
- The "Back / Continue" nav row should stack vertically on screens under 400px wide with Continue on top (primary action first).
- The progress bar is already responsive. Good.

### Email capture positioning

**Current:** Email is gated — you cannot see your report without entering an email.

**Recommended:** Show the report immediately, but with a "Get a permanent copy" email capture overlaid or placed prominently at the top and bottom of the report. Reason: gated experiences have higher abandon rates. The report itself is the best sales tool — let people see it, then capture email from the ones who want to keep it. This typically converts at a similar rate but with dramatically lower drop-off.

**Alternative (if hard gate is non-negotiable):** Add the "Skip — just show my results" link. You'll capture email from most users. The ones who skip still see the report and may convert on the CTA at the bottom.

## Part 6: Tone Calibration Notes

The current experience has two voices fighting each other:

**Voice A** — Clear, sharp, slightly provocative. "Purpose without infrastructure is a journal entry." "Not what your mind wants." This is the voice that converts. It sounds like a smart person who respects your intelligence and gets to the point.

**Voice B** — Poetic, somatic, insider. "The moment you arrived carries information your mind has never been taught to read." "Skills that live in your hands, your voice, your nervous system." This is the voice that resonates with existing clients. It sounds like a practitioner talking to someone who already speaks the language.

**The fix is not to eliminate Voice B.** It's to sequence them correctly.

The rule: **Voice A opens every screen. Voice B can appear inside the report.**

The welcome screen, instructions, questions, and CTAs should all be Voice A — clear, direct, slightly sharp. The report is where Voice B earns its place, because by then the user has invested 8 minutes and received their personalized data. They're leaning in. They'll tolerate (and appreciate) more poetic, somatic language in the context of their own results.

Currently, Voice B shows up everywhere — including places where the user hasn't committed yet. That's the tone mismatch the feedback is flagging. It's not that the poetic voice is wrong. It's that it's showing up too early.

**Practical test:** Read every line on screens 1-8 (everything before the report) aloud to someone who has never heard of human design, gene keys, or somatic work. If they furrow their brow, rewrite it.
